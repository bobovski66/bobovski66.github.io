Introduction

The rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) technologies and Large Language Models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT, Claude, and similar tools, has significantly impacted various fields, including education. These technologies offer powerful resources for learning, providing instant feedback, generating ideas, and assisting with problem-solving. However, their use also raises important questions about academic integrity, ethics, and effective learning strategies.

The purpose of this document is to outline the guidelines and best practices for the appropriate use of AI and LLMs in math courses at Centralia College. By adhering to these guidelines, students can leverage these tools to enhance their learning experience while maintaining high standards of academic honesty and responsibility.

Why Discuss AI and LLMs in an Academic Context?

AI and LLMs are increasingly accessible and widely used in educational settings. These tools have the potential to:

  • Provide personalized learning experiences by adapting to individual student needs.

  • Offer multiple explanations of mathematical and quantitative concepts, supporting deeper understanding.

  • Serve as on-demand tutors, available at any hour to answer questions and generate examples.

However, the use of AI in academics is a double-edged sword. While these technologies can be incredibly beneficial, they also pose risks:

  • Encouraging academic dishonesty by enabling easy access to answers without genuine understanding.

  • Producing confident but incorrect responses, particularly in mathematical and numerical contexts.

  • Perpetuating biases and inaccuracies present in the data used to train these models.

  • Undermining the development of critical thinking and problem-solving skills if over-relied upon.

Objectives of This Document

This document aims to:

  1. Educate students on the ethical and effective use of AI and LLMs in their studies.

  2. Provide clear guidelines on what constitutes acceptable and unacceptable use of these technologies.

  3. Encourage a balanced approach to using AI as a tool to supplement, not replace, traditional learning methods.

  4. Foster an understanding of the specific limitations AI tools have in mathematical and quantitative reasoning.

By following the guidelines set forth in this document, students will be better prepared to use AI and LLMs responsibly and effectively, thereby enhancing their educational experience while upholding the principles of academic integrity.

What Are LLMs and AI?

To understand how to use AI tools effectively in an academic setting, it is essential first to know what Large Language Models (LLMs) and artificial intelligence (AI) are, and how they function. This section provides a brief overview of these technologies and their applications in education.

Understanding Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Artificial Intelligence (AI) refers to the simulation of human intelligence processes by machines, particularly computer systems. These processes include learning (the acquisition of information and rules for using the information), reasoning (using rules to reach approximate or definite conclusions), and self-correction. AI has various subfields, including machine learning, where algorithms improve through experience, and natural language processing (NLP), which focuses on the interaction between computers and human languages.

What Are Large Language Models (LLMs)?

Large Language Models (LLMs) are a specific type of AI designed to understand, generate, and manipulate human language in a way that is contextually relevant and coherent. Examples of LLMs include ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and other similar AI assistants. These models are trained on vast amounts of text data from the internet, books, articles, and more, enabling them to generate responses based on the patterns and knowledge encoded in their training data.

How Do LLMs Work?

LLMs work by predicting the next word in a sequence of text, given all the previous words in the sequence. This is achieved through a process called transformer architecture, which allows the model to consider the context of each word in a sentence, thereby generating coherent and contextually appropriate responses. Key features of LLMs include:

  • Text Generation: LLMs can produce human-like text based on prompts provided by users. This includes writing explanations, working through example problems, and summarizing concepts.

  • Text Comprehension: These models can understand and analyze text, summarizing content, explaining concepts, or answering questions based on provided information.

  • Adaptability: LLMs can adjust their explanations to different levels of detail and different framings, making them versatile tools for learning.

AI Tools for Students to Explore

Here are some AI tools that students can use to understand their capabilities and functionalities. All of the following are free to access with a basic account:

  • ChatGPT: A widely used LLM developed by OpenAI that can generate text, answer questions, and provide explanations on a wide range of topics. Available at https://chat.openai.com/.

  • Claude: Developed by Anthropic, Claude is an AI assistant designed to be helpful, accurate, and careful about uncertainty. It is particularly useful for working through concepts and asking follow-up questions. Available at https://claude.ai/.

  • Gemini: Developed by Google DeepMind, Gemini integrates with Google's ecosystem and handles a broad range of questions and tasks. Available at https://gemini.google.com/.

  • Microsoft Copilot: Microsoft's AI assistant, built on similar technology to ChatGPT, is available for free and integrates with many tools students already use. Available at https://copilot.microsoft.com/.

  • Perplexity: An AI tool that combines language model responses with live web search, which can be useful for finding and verifying information. Available at https://www.perplexity.ai/.

No single tool is universally best. Different assistants have different strengths, and it is worth trying more than one when exploring a topic.

Capabilities and Applications of LLMs in Education

In an educational context, LLMs can serve multiple roles:

  • Personalized Tutoring: LLMs can provide tailored explanations and examples, helping students understand concepts in a way that suits their current level and learning style.

  • Interactive Learning: By engaging in dialogue with an LLM, students can ask follow-up questions, request alternative explanations, and receive immediate feedback, reinforcing learning.

  • Resource Generation: LLMs can generate practice questions, study guides, and summaries to support students outside of class time.

Limitations of LLMs and AI

While LLMs are powerful tools, they also have several important limitations that every student should understand:

  • Lack of True Understanding: LLMs do not understand content as humans do. They generate responses based on patterns in text rather than genuine comprehension of ideas.

  • Potential for Misinformation: Since LLMs are trained on a mix of reliable and unreliable sources, they may generate incorrect or misleading information, often stated with apparent confidence.

  • Mathematical Unreliability: LLMs are particularly prone to errors in numerical computation and mathematical reasoning. This is addressed in detail in Section 7.

  • Bias and Ethical Concerns: The training data for LLMs may contain biases, leading to biased or skewed responses on certain topics.

  • Over-Reliance Risks: Dependence on AI for answers can undermine critical thinking and problem-solving skills if not used carefully.

The Role of Human Judgment in Using AI Tools

It is crucial for students to use LLMs as a supplement to, not a replacement for, traditional learning methods. Human judgment is essential in interpreting the information provided by AI, verifying its accuracy, and applying it appropriately. Understanding these tools' capabilities and limitations enables students to harness their potential while maintaining academic integrity and intellectual rigor.

This foundational knowledge about AI and LLMs will help students make informed decisions about how to integrate these technologies into their learning process effectively.

Benefits of Using AI in Education

Artificial Intelligence and Large Language Models have the potential to meaningfully support learning by offering flexible, on-demand assistance. This section explores the key benefits of integrating AI tools into educational settings, particularly in mathematics and quantitative reasoning courses.

Personalized Learning Experiences

One of the most significant advantages of using AI in education is the ability to receive personalized support. AI tools can adapt to individual student needs, offering tailored explanations, examples, and practice problems based on what the student is currently working on. This personalized approach can help students:

  • Identify Knowledge Gaps: AI can help surface areas where understanding is incomplete and provide targeted explanations to address those gaps.

  • Receive Immediate Feedback: Students can get real-time responses to questions at any hour, not just during office hours or class time.

  • Customize Learning Paces: Students can slow down on difficult concepts and ask as many follow-up questions as needed without hesitation.

Enhanced Understanding of Complex Concepts

AI tools are valuable for helping students grasp challenging concepts by providing multiple explanations and alternative framings. Specific benefits include:

  • Multiple Explanatory Approaches: AI can present the same idea several different ways: through a step-by-step breakdown, a conceptual framing, a visual description, or a real-world example.

  • Simplifying Difficult Topics: AI tools can break down complex ideas into more manageable parts, making it easier to build understanding incrementally.

  • Supporting Practice: AI can generate additional examples and practice scenarios, allowing students to build familiarity through varied exposure.

Engagement Through Interactive Learning

AI and LLMs encourage active engagement with material, transforming passive reading into an interactive conversation. This can significantly enhance motivation and understanding:

  • Conversational Exploration: Students can ask follow-up questions in natural language, making it easier to probe concepts at whatever depth they need.

  • Low-Stakes Experimentation: Students can try out ideas and ask questions that might feel embarrassing in a classroom setting, without fear of judgment.

  • Active Engagement: Framing learning as a dialogue rather than a one-way delivery of information supports deeper processing and retention.

Supporting Diverse Learning Needs

AI technologies can support a diverse range of learning needs, making education more inclusive and accessible:

  • Accessibility Features: AI tools often support text-to-speech, can rephrase content in simpler terms, and can explain ideas in multiple languages, assisting students with disabilities or those learning in a second language.

  • Providing Extra Help Outside of Class: For students who need additional support between class sessions, AI can serve as a supplemental resource, available at any time.

  • Reducing Barriers to Asking for Help: Some students are reluctant to ask for clarification in class or office hours. AI provides a low-barrier alternative that can build confidence before those interactions.

Aiding in Research and Exploration

AI tools can support exploration and independent inquiry:

  • Connecting Concepts to Context: AI can help students connect abstract mathematical ideas to real-world applications, which is particularly valuable in courses like Math in Society and Introduction to Statistics.

  • Generating Discussion Prompts: AI can help students explore the implications of ideas, ask "what if" questions, and think through problems from multiple angles.

  • Fostering Curiosity: By providing easy access to explanations and examples, AI tools can encourage students to continue exploring topics beyond the immediate assignment.

By leveraging these benefits thoughtfully, students can use AI tools to enhance their learning experiences and deepen their understanding of quantitative reasoning.

Proper Use of LLMs and AI in Academic Settings

While AI tools and Large Language Models offer many benefits for enhancing learning, it is essential to use these technologies responsibly and ethically. This section provides guidelines for the appropriate use of AI and LLMs to ensure they serve as effective supplements to education without compromising academic integrity.

Using AI as a Supplementary Tool

AI should be seen as a supplement to support and deepen learning, not as a replacement for doing one's own thinking. The following guidelines can help students use AI tools effectively:

  • Enhance Understanding, Not Replace It: Use AI to clarify concepts, generate additional examples, or check your reasoning process. The goal is to understand the material yourself: AI should help you think through problems, not think for you.

  • Balance AI Use with Traditional Learning Methods: Continue to engage with your textbook, class notes, lectures, and discussions. AI is one resource among many.

  • Set Learning Goals: Before turning to an AI tool, be clear about what you want to understand. Use it to explore a specific concept or check your approach, rather than as a default first step for every problem.

Research and Understanding: Verifying Information

AI tools can provide valuable insights and explanations, but it is crucial to verify the information they provide, especially in mathematical contexts:

  • Cross-Check with Reliable Sources: Always compare AI-generated content with trusted resources, such as your textbook, course notes, or your instructor, to ensure accuracy.

  • Understand the Source of Information: Recognize that AI models generate responses based on patterns in text, which may include both reliable and unreliable sources. Apply critical thinking to evaluate the quality and accuracy of any response.

  • Use AI as a Starting Point: When exploring a new concept, treat an AI explanation as an introduction to investigate further, not as the final word. Follow up with your instructor or course materials if you have doubts.

Avoiding Plagiarism and Maintaining Academic Integrity

Maintaining academic integrity is essential. When using AI tools, it is important to ensure that all submitted work reflects your own understanding:

  • Do Not Submit AI-Generated Content as Your Own: Directly submitting content produced by an AI without meaningful engagement, understanding, or modification constitutes academic dishonesty.

  • Acknowledge AI Assistance: If you use AI to help generate ideas, explore a concept, or check your reasoning, disclose this in your work. Proper acknowledgment of AI tools is expected when they contributed meaningfully to your process.

  • Understand Course Policies: Different assignments have different expectations about AI use. When in doubt, ask your instructor before using an AI tool on a graded assignment.

Transparency: Disclosing AI Use in Your Work

Transparency about the use of AI tools is crucial for maintaining trust and integrity in an academic setting. In this course, disclosure has two required components:

  • Disclose That You Used AI: Clearly state in your submission when and how AI tools were used: for example, to clarify a concept, generate a practice problem, or explore an alternative explanation.

  • Submit the Prompt You Used: Include the actual prompt or question you entered into the AI tool as part of your submission. This is what makes disclosure meaningful: it shows exactly what you asked and allows the AI's contribution to be evaluated in context.

The AI's response then functions like any other source of information: you read it, evaluate it, and filter it through your own understanding before incorporating anything into your work. Just as you would not copy a textbook explanation word for word, you do not copy an AI response word for word. What you submit should reflect your own reasoning, informed by whatever sources you consulted.

  • Avoid Misrepresentation: Do not imply that AI-assisted work is entirely your own original thinking. Honesty about your use of AI tools, including exactly what you asked, makes it possible to give you credit for the work that genuinely is yours.

Ethical Considerations in Using AI

Using AI tools responsibly also involves considering the broader ethical implications:

  • Be Aware of Biases: AI tools may reflect biases present in their training data. Be mindful of this when using AI-generated content and critically evaluate its fairness and accuracy.

  • Respect Privacy and Security: Avoid sharing personal, sensitive, or confidential information with AI tools, as inputs may be stored or used for model training.

  • Encourage Positive Use of AI: Use AI tools to promote positive learning outcomes. Do not use them for cheating, circumventing academic policies, or generating content intended to deceive.

By following these guidelines, students can use AI tools in a way that enhances their education while upholding the values of honesty, integrity, and responsibility.

Examples of Acceptable Use

Using AI tools can be genuinely beneficial for learning when done appropriately. Below are examples of acceptable use in a mathematics course context, with sample conversations to illustrate what productive AI interaction looks like.

Brainstorming and Idea Generation

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to help identify a direction for a class project involving real-world data.

Conversation:

Student: I need to pick a topic for my math project. It has to involve collecting or analyzing data from real life. I'm not sure where to start. Can you help me think of some directions?

AI: Sure! Here are a few directions that tend to work well for data projects: you could look at how a numerical quantity people care about (like gas prices, local weather, or campus commute times) varies over time or across groups. Another option is to design a short survey about something students at your college experience, then summarize and visualize the results. A third direction is to find a public dataset on a social issue and explore what the numbers actually say versus how the issue is often described.

Student: I like the survey idea. What are some topics that would actually be interesting to other students?

AI: Topics that tend to engage people include sleep habits and academic performance, how students get to campus and how long it takes, or how often students use campus resources like tutoring or the library. All of these involve collecting real data from people you can actually reach, which makes the analysis more meaningful.

Why This Is Acceptable: The student is using AI to brainstorm starting points, not to complete the project. The thinking, data collection, and analysis still belong to the student. This kind of ideation conversation helps overcome the blank-page problem without bypassing any of the actual learning.

Clarifying Concepts Already Covered in Class

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to get a clearer explanation of a concept from class that they found confusing.

Conversation:

Student: We talked about mean and median in class and I kind of get the difference in how you calculate them, but I'm confused about when you're supposed to use one versus the other. Can you explain?

AI: Great question. The mean takes all values into account equally, which makes it sensitive to extreme values. The median just finds the middle value, so extreme values barely affect it. In practice, this matters when a dataset has outliers or a strong skew: the mean gets pulled toward the tail while the median stays put. Income is the classic example: if one person in a small group earns ten times what everyone else does, the mean income makes the group sound wealthier than most of its members actually are. The median tells you more about what a typical person earns. For symmetric data without extreme values, the two measures are close and either works.

Student: So how do you know if data has outliers or a skew before you pick a measure?

AI: That's exactly the right follow-up. You usually look at the data visually first: a histogram or dot plot will show you whether the distribution is roughly symmetric or has a long tail in one direction. If you see a strong skew or a few values far from the rest, the median is the safer choice for describing a typical value.

Why This Is Acceptable: The student is using AI to deepen their understanding of a concept covered in class, asking follow-up questions to make sense of it. This is exactly the kind of dialogue that supports learning: the student is thinking, questioning, and engaging with the idea rather than just collecting an answer.

Practice Problems and Additional Explanations

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to get additional practice and feedback on a type of problem they want to strengthen.

Conversation:

Student: I have a quiz coming up on percentages and I want more practice. Can you give me a problem?

AI: Here's one: A store marks up an item by 30%, then later puts it on sale for 20% off the marked-up price. What is the overall percentage change from the original price? Try to work through it and tell me what you get.

Student: I think the increases and decreases cancel out so it's the same price?

AI: That's a common intuition, but it doesn't quite work here because the 20% discount is applied to the already marked-up price, not the original. Let's work through it: if the original price is $100, a 30% markup brings it to $130. A 20% discount on $130 takes off $26, leaving $104. So the overall result is a 4% increase, not a return to the original price. The key idea is that percentages depend on their base: the same percentage applied to a different base gives a different dollar amount.

Student: Oh, I see. Can I try another one?

Why This Is Acceptable: The student is attempting the problem themselves and using AI to check their reasoning and learn from mistakes. The AI is acting as a practice partner, not as someone doing the work for the student. This kind of iterative feedback accelerates skill development.

Exploring Quantitative Claims and Their Implications

Example: A student uses AI to think through a quantitative claim they encountered and wants to evaluate more carefully.

Conversation:

Student: I saw a headline that said "people who eat breakfast every day earn 40% more than people who skip it." Does that mean eating breakfast causes higher income?

AI: No, and this is a really important distinction. That statistic describes a correlation: the two things tend to occur together. But correlation does not establish causation. There are likely other variables at play. People with more stable schedules, more economic security, or certain kinds of jobs may be more likely to both eat breakfast regularly and earn more. Those underlying factors could explain both behaviors without one causing the other. To claim breakfast eating causes higher income, you would need a controlled study that rules out those alternative explanations.

Student: So how do you tell when a statistic is actually showing cause and effect?

AI: The strongest evidence for causation comes from randomized controlled experiments, where people are randomly assigned to groups and the only systematic difference between them is the thing being studied. For most questions in the social sciences, those experiments are difficult or impossible to run, so researchers instead look for natural experiments or use statistical techniques to try to control for confounding variables. Even then, causation claims require careful argument, not just a correlation.

Why This Is Acceptable: The student is using AI to think more carefully about a quantitative claim from everyday life. This is precisely the kind of critical reasoning that mathematics and statistics courses are meant to develop. The AI is a thinking partner here, not a shortcut.

Examples of Unacceptable Use

While AI tools can be valuable for learning, certain uses violate academic integrity and are considered unacceptable. Below are examples of improper use, with explanations of why each is problematic.

Submitting AI-Generated Content as Original Work

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to generate a written assignment and submits it without meaningful engagement or modification.

Conversation:

Student: Can you write a two-page report explaining what statistics are and why they matter in everyday life?

AI: Sure. Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, and interpreting data. In everyday life, statistics appears in medical studies, polling, sports analysis, and financial reporting. Understanding statistics helps people evaluate claims critically, recognize when data is being misused, and make more informed decisions...

Student: (Copies the response directly into their assignment and submits it without reading, revising, or engaging with the content.)

Why This Is Unacceptable: Submitting AI-generated content as your own work is a form of academic dishonesty. It demonstrates no engagement with the material and violates academic integrity policies. The purpose of the assignment is to develop your own understanding and communication skills: outsourcing that to an AI defeats the entire point.

Relying Solely on AI Without Cross-Verification

Example: A student asks an AI a quantitative question and records the answer without checking whether it is correct or understanding the reasoning behind it.

Conversation:

Student: If the mean of a dataset is 50 and the standard deviation is 10, what percentage of data falls between 40 and 60?

AI: About 68% of the data falls within one standard deviation of the mean.

Student: (Writes down 68% and moves on without checking whether the dataset is actually normally distributed, which is the condition required for that rule to apply.)

Why This Is Unacceptable: The AI's answer is conditionally correct but incomplete: the 68% rule applies only to normally distributed data, and the AI did not flag that assumption. The student accepted an answer without understanding its conditions or verifying it applies to their situation. AI responses in mathematical and statistical contexts often contain important hidden assumptions. Always verify and understand what you are using.

Using AI to Bypass Learning or Understanding Key Concepts

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to get answers to homework problems without attempting to solve them independently.

Conversation:

Student: My homework asks: in a class of 30 students, 12 are wearing hats. What percentage of students are wearing hats? Just give me the answer.

AI: 12 divided by 30 is 0.4, which is 40%.

Student: (Copies the answer without working through the division or understanding the process.)

Why This Is Unacceptable: Using AI to retrieve answers rather than develop understanding prevents the skill-building that homework is designed to support. On a quiz or exam, the student will not have AI available and will have gained nothing from the assignment. The appropriate use would be to attempt the problem first, then use AI to check reasoning or ask for a different explanation if stuck.

Misrepresenting AI-Assisted Work as Entirely Personal Effort

Example: A student uses AI to generate the explanatory language in a written assignment but does not disclose this.

Conversation:

Student: (Asks an AI for a clear explanation of what a confidence interval means, then copies the AI's wording nearly verbatim into their assignment, presenting it as their own explanation.)

AI: A confidence interval gives a range of plausible values for a population parameter, constructed so that a specified percentage of such intervals, if repeated many times, would contain the true value...

Student: (Submits the assignment without acknowledging the AI's contribution.)

Why This Is Unacceptable: Failing to acknowledge the use of AI tools misrepresents the source of the work and violates academic integrity. Transparency about AI assistance is a basic requirement of honest academic practice. Even when AI is used appropriately, that use must be disclosed.

Using AI for Unauthorized Assistance During Assessments

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to look up answers during a quiz or exam where AI is not permitted.

Conversation:

Student: (During a closed-note quiz) A sample of 25 people has a mean age of 34. What is the standard error if the standard deviation is 10?

AI: The standard error is the standard deviation divided by the square root of the sample size: 10 divided by the square root of 25, which is 10 divided by 5, or 2.

Student: (Records this answer on the quiz.)

Why This Is Unacceptable: Using AI tools during an assessment where they are not permitted constitutes cheating and violates both course policy and the college's academic integrity standards. It also deprives the student of accurate feedback on what they actually understand, making it harder to identify and address genuine gaps before later assessments.

AI and Mathematical Reasoning: Special Considerations

Students in mathematics courses need to understand a specific and important limitation of AI tools: LLMs are not calculators, and they are not reliable mathematical reasoners. This is one of the most common sources of confusion and error when students use AI for math-related tasks.

Why LLMs Struggle with Math

LLMs work by predicting text based on patterns learned from enormous quantities of written material. They do not perform arithmetic the way a calculator does: they generate responses that look like calculations based on what correct-looking calculations tend to resemble in their training data. For straightforward, common problems, this often produces correct answers. For less routine problems, or problems requiring careful step-by-step numerical work, AI tools make errors with the same fluency and apparent confidence they use when they are correct.

This means that an AI's confident tone is not evidence of a correct answer. Students should treat AI-generated mathematical work with appropriate skepticism and always verify results independently.

Where AI Is and Is Not Reliable in Math

AI tools tend to be more reliable for conceptual and explanatory tasks than for numerical ones. The following gives a general picture:

More reliable uses:

  • Explaining what a concept means in plain language.

  • Describing the steps involved in a procedure.

  • Generating examples to think about and test your understanding.

  • Discussing the conditions under which a rule or formula applies.

  • Helping interpret the meaning of a result you have already calculated.

Less reliable uses:

  • Performing multi-step arithmetic or algebraic calculations.

  • Computing statistical values (means, standard deviations, probabilities) for specific datasets.

  • Verifying that your calculation is numerically correct.

  • Drawing conclusions from data you paste into the conversation.

What This Means in Practice

In courses like Introduction to Statistics and Math in Society, students regularly work with numbers: computing averages, interpreting percentages, reading probability claims, and evaluating data-based arguments. These are exactly the areas where AI tools are most prone to error. The practical guidance is straightforward:

  • Use AI to understand what you are supposed to do and why.

  • Do the numerical work yourself, with a calculator or by hand, and verify your results using your textbook or course materials.

  • If you ask an AI to check a calculation, treat its response as one input to evaluate, not as confirmation.

Example of a hidden error:

Student: What is the mean of the dataset: 8, 14, 21, 9, 18?

AI: To find the mean, add all the values and divide by the count. The sum is 8 + 14 + 21 + 9 + 18 = 72, and there are 5 values, so the mean is 72 / 5 = 14.4.

Student: (Copies 14.4 into their assignment without checking.)

The AI made an arithmetic error: the correct sum is 8 + 14 + 21 + 9 + 18 = 70, giving a mean of 70 / 5 = 14. The response looks exactly like a correct solution: it states the right procedure, shows its work, and arrives at a wrong answer with complete confidence. A student who checks by hand catches this immediately. A student who does not has no way of knowing the answer is wrong. Always verify numerical results against your own work.

How to Cite AI Tools in Academic Work

When using AI tools in academic work, it is important to properly acknowledge them to maintain transparency and academic integrity. This section provides guidelines on how to cite AI tools appropriately in different contexts.

General Guidelines for Citing AI Tools

Whenever you use an AI tool to assist with your work, your submission must include three things:

  • A clear statement that you used an AI tool and which one.

  • The exact prompt you entered: the actual question or instruction you gave the AI, copied as submitted.

  • A formal citation of the AI tool according to the appropriate citation style (e.g., APA, MLA, Chicago).

Including the prompt is what makes the disclosure meaningful. It allows the AI's contribution to be understood in context, and it demonstrates that you engaged with the tool intentionally rather than passively. The AI's response then functions like any other source: you read it, evaluate it, and use your own understanding to decide what is accurate and relevant before incorporating it into your work.

Citation Formats for Different Styles

Here are examples of how to cite common AI tools in standard citation styles. The format is similar across tools: give the organization, the tool name and version if known, the year, and the URL.

APA Style (ChatGPT):

OpenAI. (2025). ChatGPT (GPT-4o) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com/

APA Style (Claude):

Anthropic. (2025). Claude [Large language model]. https://claude.ai/

MLA Style (ChatGPT):

OpenAI. ChatGPT. GPT-4o, OpenAI, 2025, https://chat.openai.com/.

Chicago Style (Claude):

Anthropic. 2025. Claude. Accessed April 2025. https://claude.ai/.

Examples of Proper Attribution in Academic Work

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to brainstorm project directions.

How to Acknowledge AI Assistance:

Disclosure: I used Claude (Anthropic, 2025) to brainstorm project topic ideas.

Prompt submitted: "I need to pick a topic for my math project. It has to involve collecting or analyzing data from real life. Can you suggest some directions?"

My use of the response: The AI suggested several directions, including a survey of student commute times. I evaluated these options and selected the commute-time survey because it was feasible to carry out on campus. The project design, data collection, and analysis are my own work.

Example: A student uses an AI assistant to clarify a concept they were confused about after class.

How to Acknowledge AI Assistance:

Disclosure: I used ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2025) to help me understand when to use the mean versus the median.

Prompt submitted: "I learned about mean and median in class but I'm confused about when you're supposed to use one versus the other. Can you explain?"

My use of the response: The AI explained that the mean is sensitive to extreme values while the median is not, and gave the example of income distributions. I used this explanation to help me understand the concept, and the analysis in my assignment reflects my own interpretation of the class material and examples.

In-Text Citations and Footnotes

For in-text citations or footnotes, mention the AI tool directly where it influenced your work:

In-Text Citation Example:

According to an explanation provided by Claude (Anthropic, 2025), a confidence interval gives a range of plausible values for a population parameter rather than a single point estimate.

Footnote Example:

1. Conceptual explanation provided by ChatGPT, OpenAI, GPT-4o, 2025, https://chat.openai.com/.

Best Practices for AI Citations

  • Include the Prompt: Always submit the exact prompt you used. Paraphrasing what you asked is not sufficient: copy it as entered.

  • Describe Your Use of the Response: Explain what you took from the AI's response, what you set aside, and how you incorporated it into your own thinking. This is the part that demonstrates your engagement with the material.

  • Follow Course Guidelines: Use the citation style specified for the course and ask your instructor if you are unsure whether a particular use requires disclosure. When in doubt, disclose.

By properly citing AI tools and submitting your prompts, you uphold academic integrity, give your instructor a complete picture of your process, and make it possible to receive credit for the thinking that is genuinely yours.

Privacy and Ethical Considerations

When using AI tools, it is crucial to be mindful of privacy and ethical considerations. These tools can greatly assist in learning, but they also come with responsibilities related to data privacy and ethical use. This section provides guidelines on using AI tools responsibly.

Protecting Personal Information

AI tools operate over the internet, and any information entered may be stored, used to improve the model, or otherwise retained by the provider. To protect your privacy:

  • Do Not Share Personal Information: Avoid inputting personal details such as your full name, address, phone number, student ID, or any other sensitive information into AI tools.

  • Anonymize Data: When discussing case studies or examples in academic work, anonymize any data that relates to real people.

  • Review Privacy Policies: Before using any AI tool, review its privacy policy to understand how your data will be used and stored. This is especially important if you are considering a paid account.

Example: A student enters identifying information about another person into an AI tool without considering the privacy implications.

Scenario:

Student: My classmate Alex, who lives at 4521 Pine Street, wants to know how to approach this statistics problem. Can you help?

AI: I can definitely help with the statistics problem. For future reference, there's no need to include personal details like names and addresses when asking for academic help: the question itself is all I need.

Why This Is Unacceptable: Sharing another person's personal information with an AI tool is a privacy violation, regardless of intent. Always keep identifying details out of AI conversations.

Understanding Ethical Implications of AI Use

AI tools can have biases based on the data they were trained on, and their use can have ethical implications:

  • Be Aware of Biases: AI models may reflect biases in their training data. Be critical of the information provided and cross-check with reliable sources, particularly on topics that involve social groups, historical claims, or contested data.

  • Avoid Generating Harmful Content: Do not use AI to generate harmful, discriminatory, misleading, or offensive content.

  • Use AI Responsibly: Leverage AI tools to enhance learning and honest inquiry. Avoid using them in ways that harm others, misrepresent facts, or circumvent academic standards.

Best Practices for Ethical AI Use

  • Think Critically: Always evaluate the output from AI tools, especially when it involves numerical claims, statistical conclusions, or sensitive topics.

  • Stay Informed: Keep up with developments in AI capabilities and limitations so you can use these tools responsibly.

  • Promote Positive Use: Encourage others to use AI tools ethically and for constructive purposes.

By adhering to these guidelines, students can ensure that their use of AI tools is both ethical and respectful of privacy, contributing to a positive academic environment.

Conclusion

The integration of Large Language Models and artificial intelligence into educational settings offers genuine opportunities to support learning. When used well, these tools can provide personalized explanations, help students work through confusion, and make it easier to practice and explore ideas. They are most valuable when they function as thinking partners: prompting questions, offering alternative framings, and supporting the work of understanding rather than substituting for it.

Throughout this document, we have explored both the opportunities and the responsibilities that come with using AI in an academic context. Proper use of AI tools involves understanding their capabilities and their limitations, especially the specific limitations that apply to mathematical and quantitative reasoning. It involves maintaining academic integrity, disclosing AI assistance honestly, verifying AI-generated claims independently, and treating every numerical output from an AI with appropriate skepticism.

The Positive Impact of AI on Education

When used responsibly, AI and LLMs can:

  • Enhance Learning: AI can provide immediate feedback, offer multiple explanations of the same concept, and support students at any hour, enriching the educational process.

  • Promote Accessibility: AI tools can make education more accessible by offering support in multiple languages, adapting explanations to different levels, and providing a low-barrier way to ask questions.

  • Encourage Exploration and Curiosity: By facilitating access to explanations and generating examples to think about, AI tools can inspire students to pursue deeper understanding.

Encouragement for Responsible Use

We encourage all students to engage thoughtfully with AI tools: curious about what they can offer, honest about when and how they are used, and critical about the outputs they produce. These are the same habits of mind that make someone good at mathematics: checking your work, questioning assumptions, and not accepting an answer just because it looks plausible.

AI is a powerful tool. Used well, it can support genuine learning and expand what is possible in the time you have. Used carelessly, it can undermine the very skills you are here to build. The choice of how to use it belongs to you.

Looking Ahead

AI tools will continue to evolve, and their role in education will change alongside them. The norms around academic use of AI are still developing, and this document will be updated to reflect new understanding. What will not change is the underlying goal: to learn mathematics deeply enough that you can reason with it, evaluate quantitative claims, and apply what you know to situations you have not seen before. AI can support that goal, but it cannot achieve it for you.
