Introduction to AI and LLMs in Education

AI tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini have made powerful language-based assistance widely available. In mathematics courses, these tools offer real opportunities for learning — and real risks if used carelessly.

Why Discuss AI and LLMs in Academia?

AI tools can provide on-demand explanations, generate practice problems, and help students work through confusion outside of class. But they also pose real risks: academic dishonesty, confidently wrong answers, and the temptation to skip the thinking that makes learning stick.

Benefits of AI in Education

When used as a thinking partner rather than an answer machine, AI tools can help students identify gaps in understanding, explore concepts from multiple angles, and get support at any hour — not just during office hours.

Risks and Ethical Concerns

Understanding Artificial Intelligence (AI)

AI refers to computer systems that simulate human intelligence processes: learning, reasoning, and self-correction. Subfields include machine learning (algorithms that improve through experience) and natural language processing (understanding and generating human language).

Read More

What Are Large Language Models (LLMs)?

LLMs are AI systems trained on vast amounts of text to understand and generate human language. ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are all LLMs. They generate responses by predicting what text fits the context — not by reasoning the way a person does.

Read More

How Do LLMs Work?

LLMs predict the next word in a sequence based on patterns learned from their training data. This makes them fluent and often helpful — but it also means they can produce plausible-sounding responses that are factually or numerically wrong. Fluency is not the same as correctness.

Capabilities of LLMs in Education

  • Explaining concepts in multiple ways
  • Generating examples and practice problems
  • Answering follow-up questions conversationally
  • Helping students articulate what they don't understand

Limitations of LLMs and AI

  • No true understanding — responses are pattern-based
  • Prone to errors in arithmetic and numerical reasoning
  • May state incorrect information confidently
  • Training data may contain biases

The Role of Human Judgment

AI should supplement learning, not replace it. Every AI response should be treated the way you would treat any source: read it, evaluate it, and filter it through your own understanding before using it. Your judgment is the essential ingredient.

Read More

Acceptable Use: Brainstorming

Use AI to explore possible directions for a project. For example: asking an AI to suggest real-world data topics for a statistics project, then independently deciding which direction to pursue and doing the actual work yourself.

Read More

Acceptable Use: Clarifying Concepts

Use AI to get a different explanation of something covered in class. For example: asking an AI to explain when the mean is more informative than the median, and following up with questions until the distinction makes sense.

Read More

Acceptable Use: Practice Problems

Use AI as a practice partner — attempt a problem yourself first, then ask the AI to check your reasoning or generate a similar problem to try again. For example: working through percentage problems and asking for feedback on your approach when you get stuck.

Read More

Acceptable Use: Evaluating Quantitative Claims

Use AI to think more critically about data and statistics in the real world. For example: asking an AI to explain why a headline statistic might not mean what it appears to mean, or what would be needed to support a causal claim from correlation data.

Read More

Unacceptable Use: Submitting AI Content

Asking an AI to write or solve an assignment and submitting that output as your own work is academic dishonesty — regardless of whether you understand the result or not. The work submitted must reflect your own thinking.

Read More

Unacceptable Use: Accepting AI Answers Without Verification

Copying an AI's numerical answer without checking it is particularly risky in math courses. AI tools make arithmetic errors while showing confident, step-by-step work. The only protection is to verify the result yourself.

Read More

Unacceptable Use: Bypassing Learning

Using AI to retrieve answers to homework problems without attempting them first prevents the skill-building the assignment is designed to support. On a quiz or exam, without AI available, you will have gained nothing.

Read More

AI and Mathematical Reasoning: A Warning

LLMs are not calculators. They generate text that looks like correct mathematical work — but they make arithmetic errors, miss conditions on formulas, and produce wrong answers with apparent confidence. In statistics courses especially, always verify numerical results by hand or with a calculator.

Read More

Proper Use of AI in Academia

Use AI to enhance understanding, not replace it. Set a clear learning goal before opening an AI tool. Balance AI use with your textbook, class notes, and your own problem-solving attempts. AI is one resource among many — not the first stop for every question.

Read More

Guidelines for Ethical Use

  • Use AI to enhance understanding, not replace it
  • Always verify numerical results independently
  • Cross-check AI explanations with course materials
  • Disclose AI use and submit your prompt with your work

Read More

Disclosing and Citing AI Use

When you use an AI tool, your submission must include: (1) a statement that AI was used and which tool, (2) the exact prompt you entered, and (3) a formal citation. The AI's response is then treated like any source: read, evaluated, and filtered through your own understanding.

Conclusion: Best Practices

AI tools work best as thinking partners: tools for exploring, questioning, and understanding — not for retrieving answers. Use them honestly, verify their output, disclose their contribution, and submit your prompt. The thinking is yours.
