Coupled Oscillatory Dynamics and Hyperelliptic Geometry

This article explores the elegant interplay between physics, geometry, and topology through the dynamics of a charged particle moving through a potential energy landscape. By combining the oscillatory behavior of the particle with the magnetic field it generates, we uncover deep connections to hyperelliptic curves and their branched double covers, revealing the hidden structure of coupled dynamics.

1. Introduction

Consider a charged particle moving along the \( x \)-axis through a potential energy landscape \( P(x) \). Conservation of energy dictates:

\( H = \frac{1}{2}m(x')^2 + P(x), \quad \text{or } y^2 = P(x), \)

where \( y = x' \) is the velocity and \( P(x) \) is the potential energy function. As the particle moves, it generates a magnetic field perpendicular to its motion. Introducing \( z = ix' \) to represent this perpendicular motion, we obtain the Hamiltonian:

\( y^2 - z^2 = P(x). \)

2. 1D Potential Landscape as a Polynomial

To model the potential energy landscape, we use a polynomial function:

\( P(x) = (x - r_1)(x - r_2)\cdots(x - r_d), \)

where the roots \( r_1, r_2, \ldots, r_d \) are the turning points where the particle reverses direction. For example:

  • A quadratic potential \( P(x) = ax^2 + bx + c \) models a harmonic oscillator.
  • Higher-degree polynomials, such as \( P(x) = x(x - 1)(x + 2) \), represent multi-well landscapes with multiple oscillatory regions.

The regions where \( P(x) > 0 \) define the accessible states of the particle, while \( P(x) \leq 0 \) are energetically forbidden.

3. Hyperelliptic Curves in Phase Space

The equation \( y^2 = P(x) \) describes a hyperelliptic curve in the \( (x, y) \)-plane. At the roots of \( P(x) \), \( y = 0 \), creating branch points where the trajectory transitions between sheets. The genus \( g \) of the hyperelliptic curve is:

\( g = \lfloor (d - 1) / 2 \rfloor, \)

where \( d \) is the degree of \( P(x) \). The particle’s trajectory forms loops in phase space, oscillating between turning points.

4. Adding a Perpendicular Direction

Introducing \( z = ix' \) extends the system into 3D phase space. The equation:

\( y^2 - z^2 = P(x), \)

describes a branched double cover, where \( y^2 = P(x) \) and \( z^2 = y^2 - P(x) \) represent coupled dynamics. The total energy oscillates between the real motion (\( y^2 \)) and the perpendicular magnetic-like motion (\( z^2 \)).

5. Topology and Geometry of Branched Double Covers

The branched double cover reveals the system’s topological structure. The roots of \( P(x) = 0 \) define branch points, and the genus determines the number of independent oscillatory modes. The surface can be decomposed into tubes, each representing an oscillatory region where energy flows between real and perpendicular components.
