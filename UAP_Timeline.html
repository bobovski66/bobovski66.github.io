Tufts biologist Michael Levin's multi-competency architecture argues that cognition, agency, and goal-directedness are substrate-independent — they emerge at any scale where a system integrates information, maintains a self-model, and pursues goals across time. THE XENOBOT → ANTHROBOT SEQUENCE — NHI MADE IN THE LAB

Xenobots (2020–2021, PNAS): Living robots built from African clawed frog (Xenopus laevis) embryonic skin and heart cells. No genetic modification. The cells, freed from their developmental context, spontaneously assembled into novel body plans never seen in nature — navigated passageways, collected material, healed themselves, and crucially: kinematically self-replicated, gathering loose cells and compressing them into new xenobots. A behavior with no analog anywhere in the frog genome. The organism's DNA said "make a frog." The cells, given freedom, made something else entirely.

Anthrobots (2023, Advanced Science): The same experiment, but with adult human tracheal cells. No embryonic material. No genetic engineering. No scaffolding or surgical shaping — they self-assemble spontaneously when given the right chemical environment. The result: tiny spherical organisms, 100–1000 cells, covered in beating cilia, capable of autonomous locomotion. Genome: 100% Homo sapiens. Morphology: nothing that has ever existed in human evolutionary history. When clustered into "superbots," they moved into artificial neural wounds in lab dishes and induced regrowth — bridging the gap with new neurons. The mechanism of healing remains unknown.

Anthrobot lifecycle study (2025, Advanced Science): The deepest finding yet. When Levin and Gumuskaya examined the gene expression profile of anthrobots, they found the cells had become biologically younger — rolling back their epigenetic clock without any genetic reprogramming, purely through the act of reorganizing into a new collective form. More striking: they re-expressed ancient genes shared with organisms as far back as single-celled ancestors, and embryonic genes for body axis patterning (anterior-posterior, dorsal-ventral) that are normally only active in the earliest moments of development. The cells were not just building a new body plan. They were remembering an older, deeper, more general form of being.

His concept of the "cognitive light cone" formalizes this: every agent operates at a characteristic scale of integration — the spatiotemporal range over which it forms coherent goals and acts.
